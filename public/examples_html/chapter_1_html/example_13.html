Let \( A = \{1, 2, 3\} \), \( B = \{4, 5, 6, 7\} \) and \( f = \{(1, 4), (2, 5), (3, 6)\} \) be a function from \( A \) to \( B \). Show that \( f \) is one-one but not onto function.

1

Given the sets \( A = \{1, 2, 3\} \), \( B = \{4, 5, 6, 7\} \) and the function \( f = \{(1, 4), (2, 5), (3, 6)\} \).

2

To show \( f \) is one-one, we check if distinct elements in \( A \) have distinct images in \( B \). Here, \( f(1) = 4 \), \( f(2) = 5 \), and \( f(3) = 6 \), all outputs are unique.

3

To check if \( f \) is onto, verify if every element in \( B \) has a pre-image in \( A \). The element \( 7 \in B \) has no pre-image in \( A \), so \( f \) is not onto.

\( f \) is one-one but not an onto function.
