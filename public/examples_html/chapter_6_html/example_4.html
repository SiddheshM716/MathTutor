Prove that \(\sec \theta - \cos \theta = \tan \theta \sin \theta\)

1

Express \(\sec \theta\) in terms of \(\cos \theta\):

\[

\sec \theta = \frac{1}{\cos \theta}

\]

2

Substitute \(\sec \theta\) into the left-hand side (LHS) of the equation:

\[

\sec \theta - \cos \theta = \frac{1}{\cos \theta} - \cos \theta

\]

3

Combine the terms over a common denominator:

\[

\frac{1 - \cos^2 \theta}{\cos \theta}

\]

4

Use the Pythagorean identity \(1 - \cos^2 \theta = \sin^2 \theta\):

\[

\frac{\sin^2 \theta}{\cos \theta}

\]

5

Rewrite \(\frac{\sin^2 \theta}{\cos \theta}\) as \(\sin \theta \cdot \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}\):

\[

\sin \theta \cdot \tan \theta

\]

\(\tan \theta \sin \theta\)
