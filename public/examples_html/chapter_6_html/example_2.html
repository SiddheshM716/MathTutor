Prove that \(\frac{1 - \cos A}{1 + \cos A} = \left(\frac{\sin A}{1 + \cos A}\right)^2\)

1

Multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate of \(1 + \cos A\), which is \(1 - \cos A\).

\[

\frac{1 - \cos A}{1 + \cos A} \cdot \frac{1 - \cos A}{1 - \cos A} = \frac{(1 - \cos A)^2}{(1 + \cos A)(1 - \cos A)}

\]

2

Simplify the denominator using the identity \(1 - \cos^2 A = \sin^2 A\).

\[

\frac{(1 - \cos A)^2}{1 - \cos^2 A} = \frac{(1 - \cos A)^2}{\sin^2 A}

\]

3

Rewrite the expression as a square and simplify.

\[

\left(\frac{1 - \cos A}{\sin A}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{\sin A}{1 + \cos A}\right)^2

\]

\(\frac{1 - \cos A}{1 + \cos A} = \left(\frac{\sin A}{1 + \cos A}\right)^2\) is proven.
