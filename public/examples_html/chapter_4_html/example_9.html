Two poles of height ‘a’ metres and ‘b’ metres are ‘p’ metres apart. Prove that the height of the point of intersection of the lines joining the top of each pole to the foot of the opposite pole is given by \(\frac{ab}{a+b}\) metres.

1

Let AB and CD be two poles of height ‘a’ metres and ‘b’ metres respectively, placed ‘p’ metres apart (AC = p). Let the lines AD and BC intersect at point O, with OL = h metres being the height of the intersection point.

2

Assign CL = x and LA = y, so \(x + y = p\).

3

In \(\triangle ABC\) and \(\triangle LOC\), \(\angle CAB = \angle CLO = 90°\). By AA similarity, \(\triangle CAB \sim \triangle CLO\).

Using the similarity ratio: \(\frac{CA}{CL} = \frac{AB}{LO}\), we get \(\frac{p}{x} = \frac{a}{h}\). Solving for \(x\) gives \(x = \frac{ph}{a}\) (Equation 1).

4

In \(\triangle ALO\) and \(\triangle ACD\), \(\angle ALO = \angle ACD = 90°\) and \(\angle A\) is common. By AA similarity, \(\triangle ALO \sim \triangle ACD\).

Using the similarity ratio: \(\frac{AL}{AC} = \frac{OL}{DC}\), we get \(\frac{y}{p} = \frac{h}{b}\). Solving for \(y\) gives \(y = \frac{ph}{b}\) (Equation 2).

5

Add Equations 1 and 2: \(x + y = \frac{ph}{a} + \frac{ph}{b}\). Since \(x + y = p\), substitute to get \(p = ph \left(\frac{1}{a} + \frac{1}{b}\right)\).

6

Simplify the equation: \(\frac{1}{h} = \frac{1}{a} + \frac{1}{b}\).

Solving for \(h\) gives the height of the intersection point as \(h = \frac{ab}{a + b}\) metres.
