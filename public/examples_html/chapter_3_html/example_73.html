If \( A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \) and \( B = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 4 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \), show that \( (AB)^T = B^T A^T \).

1

Compute the product \( AB \).

The formula for matrix multiplication is \( (AB)_{ij} = \sum_{k} A_{ik} B_{kj} \).

\( AB = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 4 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \times (-1) + 2 \times 1 & 1 \times 4 + 2 \times 1 \\ 0 \times (-1) + 2 \times 1 & 0 \times 4 + 2 \times 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 6 \\ 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \).

2

Compute the transpose of \( AB \), denoted \( (AB)^T \).

The transpose of a matrix swaps its rows and columns.

\( (AB)^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 6 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \).

3

Compute the transposes \( A^T \) and \( B^T \).

\( A^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \).

\( B^T = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \).

4

Compute the product \( B^T A^T \).

Using matrix multiplication again:

\( B^T A^T = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \times 1 + 1 \times 2 & -1 \times 0 + 1 \times 2 \\ 4 \times 1 + 1 \times 2 & 4 \times 0 + 1 \times 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 6 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \).

Since \( (AB)^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 6 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \) and \( B^T A^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 6 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \), we have shown that \( (AB)^T = B^T A^T \).
