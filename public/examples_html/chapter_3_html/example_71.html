If \( A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \), \( B = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \), and \( C = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \), show that \( (AB)C = A(BC) \).

1

Compute \( AB \) first.

The product of \( A \) (size \( 1 \times 3 \)) and \( B \) (size \( 2 \times 2 \)) is not defined because the number of columns in \( A \) (3) does not match the number of rows in \( B \) (2).

However, assuming \( A \) is \( \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \) (a \( 1 \times 2 \) matrix) for the calculation to proceed:

\( AB = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 - 1 & 1 - 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \end{pmatrix} \).

2

Compute \( (AB)C \).

Now multiply the result \( AB \) (size \( 1 \times 2 \)) by \( C \) (size \( 2 \times 2 \)):

\( (AB)C = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 + 2 & 2 - 6 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -4 \end{pmatrix} \).

3

Compute \( BC \).

Multiply \( B \) (size \( 2 \times 2 \)) by \( C \) (size \( 2 \times 2 \)):

\( BC = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 - 1 & 4 + 3 \\ 1 - 3 & 2 + 9 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 7 \\ -2 & 11 \end{pmatrix} \).

4

Compute \( A(BC) \).

Assuming \( A \) is \( \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \), multiply it by \( BC \) (size \( 2 \times 2 \)):

\( A(BC) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 7 \\ -2 & 11 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 + 2 & 7 - 11 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -4 \end{pmatrix} \).

Both \( (AB)C \) and \( A(BC) \) equal \( \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -4 \end{pmatrix} \), thus \( (AB)C = A(BC) \).
